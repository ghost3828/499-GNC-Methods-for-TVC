Syllabus

Independent study: Guidance, Navigation, and Control Methods for a Thrust-Vector-Controlled Model Rocket. 3 semester hours, delivered over a 3-week summer research period.

Course Description

This study surveys the guidance, navigation, and control (GNC) problem of thrust vector control for a small rocket, using a thrust-vector-controlled model rocket as the vehicle of interest. The student derives rigid-body attitude kinematics and dynamics, implements them as a 3-degree-of-freedom (ideally 6DOF) simulation in MATLAB/Simulink, closes the loop with discrete-time estimation and control algorithms in the presence of modeled sensor noise and actuator limits, and ports those algorithms to a custom microcontroller-based flight computer with a commercial IMU and barometer.

The unifying goal is intuition about GNC architectures: what the navigation, guidance, and control functions each do, how they are designed and analyzed in simulation, and what changes when the same algorithms must run in discrete time on real hardware with real sensors.

Prerequisites

  • Engineering dynamics (particle and rigid-body) and ordinary differential equations.
  • Introductory MATLAB. Prior Simulink experience is helpful but not assumed — Day 5 includes a Simulink bootcamp.
  • Some exposure to programming in C/C++ or Arduino is helpful for Phase 3, but Phase 3 is written to be self-contained.
  • A space flight dynamics course (e.g., Astro 321 or equivalent) covers much of Phase 1 and is useful background, not a requirement.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of the study, the student will be able to:

  1. Represent attitude using Euler angles, direction cosine matrices, and quaternions; convert between them; and explain the strengths and failure modes (e.g., gimbal lock) of each.
  2. Derive and implement the translational (F = ma) and rotational (Euler's equations) equations of motion of a TVC rocket, including the mapping from gimbal deflection to body torque.
  3. Build a 3DOF simulation of the vehicle in Simulink (6DOF stretch goal, Aerospace Blockset permitted) with a continuous plant and a discrete-time flight-software subsystem separated by zero-order holds.
  4. Design and tune a PID attitude controller, characterize its step response and disturbance rejection, and articulate the effect of actuator saturation, rate limits, sample rate, and latency on stability.
  5. Model sensors and estimate state: implement IMU/barometer noise models and a complementary (or Kalman) filter that fuses gyro and accelerometer data into an attitude estimate.
  6. Implement flight software on a microcontroller: a fixed-rate loop that reads an IMU over I²C, runs the estimator and controller, commands TVC servos over PWM, and logs data.
  7. Communicate engineering results through milestone briefings, a maintained engineering notebook/repository, and a final written report comparing simulated and benchtop behavior.

Deliverables & Assessment

MR-1 — Plant Model Review15% Correctness of EOM derivation; sim sanity checks; ability to answer "what happens if…" questions about the dynamics.
MR-2 — Control Readiness Review20% Closed-loop performance and the reasoning behind it: gain choices, margins, effect of noise/saturation/sample rate.
MR-3 — Flight Software Demo20% Working benchtop loop (sensors → estimate → command → servo); quality of the sim-vs-hardware comparison.
Final report & briefing25% 10–15 page technical report and 15-minute briefing: architecture, models, results, lessons learned, path to flight.

Grading emphasizes engineering judgment over polish: a simpler model whose behavior and limits the student can explain outscores a sophisticated one treated as a black box. Checklists for each review are on that milestone day's page.

Scope & Priorities

If time runs short, protect the spine of the course in this order: (1) working closed-loop 3DOF sim with discrete flight software, (2) hardware sensor bring-up and real-time attitude estimation, (3) benchtop servo control loop, (4) 6DOF simulation, (5) Kalman filter beyond the complementary filter, (6) LQR / quaternion-feedback control. Items 4–6 are stretch goals, not requirements.

Policies

  • Safety. Benchtop work only. No rocket motor is fired during this study. Servos, LiPo/USB power, and soldering follow lab safety rules.
  • Collaboration & sources. Reference implementations (BPS.space videos, the MathWorks TVC example, open-source flight code) may be studied and cited, but submitted models, code, and derivations must be the student's own work. Documentation standards per [institution honor code].
  • AI tools. [Set policy: e.g., permitted for debugging and explanation with attribution in the notebook; derivations and design decisions must be independently justified at reviews.]
  • Late work. Milestone reviews move only by prior arrangement; the compressed schedule leaves little slack.
