Phase 03 · Flight days 11–15 · Milestone MR-3

Flight Software & Hardware

Simulation is where GNC is designed; hardware is where it's proven. This week you rebuild your discrete flight software on an Arduino-class microcontroller — same estimator, same controller, same 100 Hz loop — and close the loop on the bench with a real IMU and real servos. The differences you find between sim and bench are the whole lesson.

Phase goal A flight computer that fuses sensor data to estimate the rocket's attitude and generates control commands for the TVC mount in real time — demonstrated on the bench and compared quantitatively against the Phase 2 simulation.
Before you start All parts on the hardware page should be in hand: microcontroller, ISM330DHCX IMU, BMP581 barometer, Qwiic cables, hobby servos, and (ideally) a printed TVC mount. If parts slip, Days 11–13 can run on the microcontroller + IMU alone.
DAY11

Arduino Foundations & Loop Scheduling

Flight software is a loop that must never be late. Learn to write non-blocking code before touching a single sensor.

Objectives

  • Set up the toolchain, blink an LED, and use the serial monitor/plotter as your telemetry window.
  • Internalize the non-blocking pattern: never delay() in flight code. Schedule work off millis()/micros().
  • Build the flight-software skeleton: a main loop that runs the GNC task at exactly 100 Hz, mirrors your Simulink Flight Software subsystem (read → estimate → control → command → log), and measures its own timing.
  • Connect the concept: the fixed loop period on the Arduino is the sample time of your discrete controller — the ZOH from Day 7 made real.

Do

  • Work through Blink Without Delay, then extend it: three LEDs at three unrelated rates, no delay().
  • Write fsw_skeleton.ino: a 100 Hz scheduler with stubbed estimate/control functions, printing loop rate and worst-case overrun. Prove it holds rate while printing telemetry.

Read / Watch

  • Start here Arduino — Blink Without Delay
  • arduino.cc — language reference as needed
Deliverablefsw_skeleton.ino holding 100 Hz with timing telemetry; notebook entry mapping each Simulink block to a function in the skeleton.
DAY12

Sensor Bring-Up: IMU + Barometer over I²C

Navigation inputs. Get clean, correctly-scaled, correctly-signed data before you trust a single computed angle.

Objectives

  • Understand the two serial buses you're using: I²C (sensors, shared two-wire bus, addresses) and UART/Serial (telemetry to the laptop).
  • Bring up the ISM330DHCX (gyro + accel) and BMP581 (baro) on the Qwiic bus; configure ranges and output data rates deliberately (gyro range must cover expected body rates; ODR ≥ your loop rate).
  • Verify axes and signs against the datasheet: physically rotate the board about each axis and confirm the sign convention matches the body frame in your sim. Sign errors here are the classic "controller fights itself" bug.
  • Characterize the real sensors: log 60 s of stationary data; compute gyro bias and noise standard deviation; compare against the datasheet numbers you used in the Day 8 sim.

Do

  • Integrate sensor reads into the 100 Hz skeleton (no blocking waits — check data-ready).
  • Log data: stream CSV over serial and capture to file, and/or write to SD/flash if your board supports it. Every bench test from now on gets logged.
  • Update your Simulink sensor model with the measured noise/bias values. The sim and the hardware should now share one truth.

Read / Watch

  • SparkFun hookup guides for the ISM330DHCX and BMP581 (linked from each product page)
DeliverableLive gyro/accel/baro telemetry at loop rate; stationary-log characterization table (bias, σ) vs. datasheet; sim noise model updated.
DAY13

Real-Time Attitude Estimation on Hardware

Port the Day 9 estimator. The board should know which way is up — and keep knowing it while you wave it around.

Objectives

  • Implement quaternion integration of the gyro at 100 Hz (fixed-step, renormalize each cycle) — the discrete twin of your Day 3 script.
  • Add startup gyro-bias calibration: average N samples while stationary, subtract thereafter. Quantify the drift improvement.
  • Implement the complementary filter correction from the accelerometer (valid on the bench, where specific force ≈ gravity).
  • Validate against physical truth: rest the board at known angles (level, 30°, 45°, 90° using a protractor/angle block) and record estimator error; do fast slews and confirm it recovers.

Do

  • Port your estimator into the skeleton. Writing the quaternion math yourself is preferred; if you adopt the Orientation library, first verify it against your own MATLAB results — it's untested by the instructor.
  • Stream estimated Euler angles to the serial plotter; capture the known-angle validation table.
  • Run the drift test: 5 minutes stationary — how far does the estimate wander with and without the accelerometer correction?

Read / Watch

  • Re-read the TKJ Kalman/complementary filter tutorial — this time as an implementer
DeliverableEstimator running at 100 Hz on hardware; known-angle validation table; drift comparison plot.
DAY14

Servos, Controller Port & the Benchtop Closed Loop

Actuation, then the moment of truth: tilt the vehicle and watch the gimbal push back.

Objectives

  • Drive hobby servos with the Servo library / PWM; map gimbal angle δ ↔ servo microseconds, including linkage geometry, direction, and software travel limits that protect the mount.
  • Port the Day 7 discrete PID — identical gains, sample rate, saturation, and anti-windup — from Simulink into C.
  • Close the loop on the bench: mount the flight computer on (or with) the TVC assembly, command "vertical," and demonstrate that tilting the assembly drives a correcting gimbal deflection in the right direction, promptly, without oscillation.
  • Compare against sim: apply an attitude step on the bench, log the response, and overlay it on the equivalent Simulink run. Explain the differences (servo lag? linkage slop? estimator lag? loop jitter?).

Do

  • Servo checkout first with sensors out of the loop: sweep commands, verify limits and center.
  • Then close the loop. Debug with the Day 10 suspect list. If it oscillates or diverges, that's Exhibit A for the report — diagnose it like an engineer, in the log data.
  • Rehearse the MR-3 demo end-to-end once today, not tomorrow.

Read / Watch

  • BPS.space Scout F — TVC hardware section

DAY15

Final Demo, Briefing & Report

Show it, defend it, write it down so the next cadet can pick it up.

Objectives

  • Deliver MR-3: live bench demo plus a 15-minute briefing covering architecture, models, results, and the sim-vs-hardware comparison.
  • Submit the final report (10–15 pages): GNC architecture; dynamics derivation; sim design and verification; control design and tuning rationale; estimation design; hardware implementation; results; lessons learned; and a concrete "path to flight" section — what remains before this vehicle could attempt free flight (6DOF validation, ignition/abort logic, structural work, range safety).
  • Leave the repository in a state a future student could clone and continue: README, parameter files, wiring notes, and the notebook complete.

Milestone Review · Day 15

MR-3 — Flight Software Demo

Ready when you can check every box:

  • Flight computer holds its loop rate with sensors, estimator, controller, servo output, and logging all active.
  • Estimator validated at known angles and characterized for drift.
  • Live demo: tilt the assembly, gimbal corrects — correct direction, sensible magnitude, stable.
  • Bench-vs-sim step-response overlay with a defensible explanation of every visible difference.
  • Final report and briefing delivered; repository handoff-ready.
  • Can answer: What surprised you most going from sim to hardware? Which single model improvement would most reduce the sim/bench gap? What is the very next test on the path to a real flight?
