Phase 02 · Flight days 06–10 · Milestone MR-2

Closing the Loop

Your plant tumbles on its own — now make it stand up. This week adds each real-world layer one at a time: a controller, then actuator limits, then discrete time, then noisy sensors, then an estimator. Adding them in order teaches you exactly which effect causes which symptom — the core intuition this course exists to build.

Phase goal A closed-loop simulation in which discrete-time flight software (estimator + PID controller, running through zero-order holds at a fixed sample rate) stabilizes the vehicle using noisy modeled sensors and a rate/angle-limited servo — the exact software you will port to hardware in Phase 3.
DAY06

PID Control — First Closed Loop

Close the loop on the ideal plant with perfect state knowledge. Learn what each gain buys you and what it costs.

Objectives

  • Explain the P, I, and D terms physically for attitude control: P = spring stiffness on angle error, D = damping on angle rate, I = trim against steady disturbances (e.g., motor misalignment, CG offset).
  • Tune a PD (then PID) controller mapping pitch error → gimbal command, and characterize it: rise time, overshoot, settling time, disturbance rejection.
  • Show the classic failure modes on purpose: too much P (oscillation), too much D (noise amplification — foreshadowing Day 8), too much I (windup and slow oscillation).

Do

  • Add a Controller block inside Flight Software (continuous for today). Feed it true attitude from the plant.
  • Test cases: (a) start 5° off vertical, command vertical; (b) step attitude command of 5° mid-burn; (c) impulse disturbance torque ("wind gust") — plot θ and δ for each.
  • Tuning study: build a small table of gain sets vs. response metrics. Keep it — it goes in the MR-2 package and the final report.

Read / Watch

  • Start here Understanding PID Control — Brian Douglas, MATLAB Tech Talks (episodes 1–4 today)
  • Brian Douglas — Control System Lectures (reference as needed)
  • TVC Rocket PID Tuning in Simulink (compare their tuning process to yours)
DeliverableClosed-loop model on the ideal plant; gain-tuning table with response plots for the three test cases.
DAY07

Actuators, Limits & Discrete-Time Flight Software

Real servos are slow, limited, and sampled. Restructure the model so the flight software runs the way it will on the Arduino.

Objectives

  • Model the TVC servo: angle saturation (±5°), rate limit, and a first-order lag or transport delay. Observe the phase lag it adds and what that does to your margins.
  • Explain continuous vs. discrete time: the analysis lives in continuous time, but everything on a microcontroller is discrete. Convert your controller to run at a fixed sample rate (start at 100 Hz) behind zero-order holds.
  • Find the breaking point: lower the sample rate (100 → 50 → 25 → 10 Hz) until the loop destabilizes, and connect that to the phase lag of sampling (≈ half a sample period plus compute delay).
  • Implement anti-windup: clamp or back-calculate the integrator when the gimbal saturates.

Do

  • Restructure the top level per the recommended architecture: continuous Plant + Actuator, discrete Flight Software subsystem sampled through ZOH blocks. This structure is non-negotiable — it is what makes the Phase 3 port honest.
  • Re-tune gains for the discrete, saturated loop. Document what changed and why.
  • Demonstrate windup with and without your anti-windup fix during a large attitude command.

Read / Watch

  • Start here Improving the Beginner's PID — Brett Beauregard (read the whole series; it is exactly the discrete-implementation wisdom you need)
  • Understanding PID Control — remaining episodes (anti-windup, derivative filtering)
DeliverableDiscrete-time flight-software subsystem @ 100 Hz with ZOH; sample-rate sweep plot; anti-windup demonstration.
DAY08

Sensor Models

Your controller has been reading truth. Real flight computers never get truth — they get an IMU.

Objectives

  • Describe what each sensor measures: 3-axis gyro → angular velocity (body frame); 3-axis accelerometer → specific force, not acceleration (it reads +1 g sitting still and ~0 in free fall — reason through why); barometer → pressure → altitude AGL.
  • Model MEMS gyro errors: white noise, constant bias, (optionally) bias random walk. Show how integrating a biased gyro makes attitude error grow linearly with time — the drift problem Day 9 solves.
  • Model barometer noise/quantization and its slower update rate.

Do

  • Build the Sensors subsystem: truth in → noisy, biased, sampled measurements out. Parameterize noise levels from the ISM330DHCX and BMP581 datasheets (see hardware).
  • Feed raw gyro-integrated attitude to your controller and watch performance degrade; quantify the attitude drift rate for your chosen bias.
  • Notebook entry: which sensor errors matter most for a 10–30 second flight, and which can you ignore?

Read / Watch

  • Start here A Simple MEMS Gyro Model Using MATLAB/Simulink
  • Understanding Sensor Fusion and Tracking — MATLAB Tech Talks (episodes 1–2)
DeliverableSensor subsystem with datasheet-derived noise parameters; gyro-drift quantification plot.
DAY09

State Estimation

Navigation, finally: fuse a drifty-but-smooth gyro with a noisy-but-unbiased accelerometer into an attitude estimate the controller can trust.

Objectives

  • Implement a complementary filter: high-pass the gyro-integrated attitude, low-pass the accelerometer gravity-vector attitude, and blend. Understand the single tuning knob (crossover frequency) and its trade.
  • Understand the Kalman filter conceptually: predict with a model, correct with measurements, weight by relative uncertainty. Implement the classic 1D angle/bias KF from the TKJ tutorial if time permits.
  • Know the caveat: during powered flight the accelerometer measures thrust, not just gravity — so the correction is only trustworthy pre-launch and in benign conditions. (This is why real rockets align on the pad, then trust the gyro through the burn.)

Do

  • Add an Estimator block inside Flight Software, upstream of the controller. Same sample rate, same discrete style.
  • Plot estimated vs. true attitude through a full simulated flight; report RMS estimation error for three noise levels.
  • Swap the controller's input from truth to estimate. Re-verify the Day 6 test cases — this is the honest closed loop.

Read / Watch

  • Start here A Practical Approach to Kalman Filter and How to Implement It — TKJ Electronics
  • Understanding Sensor Fusion and Tracking — remaining episodes
  • Steve Brunton — Control Bootcamp (deeper theory, reference as needed)
DeliverableEstimator in the loop; estimate-vs-truth plots with RMS error; closed loop verified on estimated state.
DAY10

Integration, Robustness & the 6DOF Stretch

Everything at once — then find out how wrong your parameters can be before the design breaks.

Objectives

  • Run the full stack end-to-end: noisy sensors → estimator → PID → servo model → plant, all flight software discrete at 100 Hz.
  • Robustness study: vary mass, CG location, moment of inertia, and thrust ±10–20% (a lightweight Monte Carlo — a for-loop over randomized parameters counts). Report the fraction of runs that stay stable and the worst cases.
  • Stretch Extend to 6DOF: quaternion attitude, two-axis gimbal (pitch + yaw), using the Aerospace Blockset 6DOF (quaternion) block. Reuse everything — that's the payoff of clean architecture.
  • Stretch Read ahead on LQR and quaternion-feedback control as alternatives to PID; one paragraph in the notebook on when you'd bother.

Do

  • Freeze a "flight configuration": gains, rates, filter constants, all recorded in vehicle_params.m. This exact configuration is what gets ported to the Arduino.
  • Prepare the MR-2 package: architecture diagram, tuning history, robustness results, and a nominal-flight movie/animation if time allows.

Read / Watch

  • MathWorks TVC rocket example — compare your final architecture against theirs; note the differences deliberately.
  • Shared folder Example TVC simulation code (1.References\2. Example Code) — instructor's caveat applies: no guarantee these are correct or best practice. Critique them; finding a flaw is fair game at MR-2.

Milestone Review · Day 10

MR-2 — Control Readiness Review

The center-of-gravity of the course. Ready when you can check every box:

  • Closed-loop sim with discrete flight software (ZOH, fixed rate), limited servo, noisy sensors, estimator in the loop.
  • Gain-tuning history with quantified step response and disturbance rejection.
  • Sample-rate sweep showing where and why the loop destabilizes.
  • Estimator performance quantified (RMS error vs. truth) and the accelerometer-under-thrust caveat explained.
  • Robustness study across parameter dispersion, with worst cases identified.
  • Can answer: Why does derivative gain amplify sensor noise, and what's the standard fix? What limits how low the sample rate can go? If the vehicle oscillates at 2 Hz on the bench next week, what are your first three suspects?
