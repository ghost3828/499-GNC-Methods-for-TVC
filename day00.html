Prerequisite Refresher

Not one of the 15 graded days — a self-paced diagnostic to shore up the math and physics Day 1 assumes you already have: vectors, matrices, and Newtonian mechanics.

How to use this day Day 1 opens by deriving a GNC loop, and by Day 2 you're building rotation matrices by hand. Nothing here is new material — it's a refresher and a diagnostic. Work the problem set below; anywhere you get stuck or feel shaky is exactly where to spend an extra hour before Day 1 starts. If everything below is comfortable, skip straight to Day 1.
DAY00

Objectives

  • Vectors: compute dot products, cross products, magnitudes, and unit vectors by hand, and explain each result geometrically (dot → projection/angle, cross → perpendicular vector whose magnitude is an area).
  • Linear algebra: multiply matrices and vectors, transpose and invert a small matrix, and explain what an orthogonal matrix is — a rotation matrix (DCM) is exactly that, and you'll build one by hand on Day 2.
  • Newtonian mechanics: draw a free-body diagram, apply F = ma, compute torque as τ = r × F, and state the rotational analog τ = Iα.
  • Differential equations: recognize a first- vs. second-order ODE, and explain — at the level of "the slope tells you the next step" — what numerical integration (Euler's method) does. This is what Simulink is doing under the hood on Day 5.
Primer Everything this course builds — attitude kinematics, rigid-body dynamics, a Simulink model, an embedded estimator — is assembled out of four ingredients you likely already have from prior coursework: vectors, matrices, F = ma, and the idea of an ODE. Vectors are how you'll represent position, velocity, angular rate, and force, and the two products you need are the dot product (how much two vectors point the same way — it's how you'll project gravity onto a body axis) and the cross product (the vector perpendicular to two others — it's how torque and angular momentum are defined). Linear algebra shows up because a rotation is a linear transformation: multiplying a vector by a 3×3 matrix rotates it from one frame to another, and the special property that makes a matrix a valid rotation — orthogonality, Rᵀ = R⁻¹ — is the exact check you'll run on your first hand-built DCM on Day 2. Newtonian mechanics supplies the physics: F = ma for how the vehicle translates, and its rotational twin, torque and moment of inertia, for how it tumbles — which is the whole reason a TVC rocket needs a controller in the first place. And every one of those laws is a differential equation: a rule for how fast something changes right now, which is exactly what a Simulink integrator block evaluates at each time step. None of this is deep — it's the same four ideas from your dynamics and math courses, just about to get a lot of repetition. This day exists so a shaky spot in any of them doesn't quietly turn into a confusing bug three weeks from now.

Do

  • Work the self-check problem set below on paper. Don't use a calculator or MATLAB for the vector/matrix problems — the point is to rebuild hand fluency, not just get the right number.
    • Vectors: given a = [1, 2, 2] and b = [0, 1, -1], compute a·b, a×b, |a|, and the unit vector along a. Then explain in one sentence what a·b = 0 would have told you.
    • Matrices: multiply a 3×3 rotation-like matrix (any matrix with orthonormal rows works, e.g. a 90° rotation about z) by a vector by hand; then transpose the matrix and multiply again. Confirm the second result "undoes" the first.
    • Mechanics: a 1 m rod pivoted at one end has a 5 N force applied perpendicular to it at the far end. Compute the torque about the pivot, and — given a rod moment of inertia I — write the resulting angular acceleration.
    • ODEs: given dx/dt = -2x, x(0) = 10, solve it analytically, then hand-propagate three steps of Euler's method with Δt = 0.1 and compare to the analytical answer at t = 0.3.
  • MATLAB warm-up: define two vectors and a 3×3 matrix, and reproduce the dot product, cross product (cross()), matrix-vector multiply, and transpose (') from above. This is the same MATLAB fluency Day 2's euler2dcm function will assume.
  • If any objective above felt unfamiliar rather than rusty, read or watch the corresponding item below before Day 1.
