Coordinate Frames, Euler Angles & DCMs

Attitude is the relationship between two frames. Get rigorous about which frames, and how to write that relationship down.

DAY02

Objectives

  • Define the body frame (fixed to the rocket) and the navigation/world/inertial frame (fixed to the launch site), and state which quantities live in which frame (gyro rates: body; gravity: world; thrust: body).
  • Build a rotation matrix (direction cosine matrix, DCM) from a sequence of Euler rotations; verify it is orthonormal (R·Rᵀ = I, det = 1).
  • Demonstrate gimbal lock: find the Euler-angle configuration where a degree of freedom disappears, and explain why that matters for a rocket that may pitch through 90°.
Primer Attitude has no meaning in isolation — it is always the orientation of one frame relative to another, so the first discipline of this course is bookkeeping: know which frame every vector lives in before you write a single equation. The body frame rides with the rocket (its x-axis typically along the roll axis); the inertial/navigation frame is fixed to the launch site. A gyro reads angular rate in the body frame; gravity naturally points "down" in the inertial frame; you need both to reason about the vehicle. The direction cosine matrix is the tool that moves a vector between frames — nine numbers, only three of which are independent, built from a sequence of elementary rotations (Euler angles). Euler angles are intuitive (roll, pitch, yaw) but hide a trap: at certain orientations two of the three rotation axes become parallel and a degree of freedom vanishes — gimbal lock. For a rocket that pitches through 90° during a gravity turn, that trap is not a corner case, it's the flight profile. Today you build and test the DCM machinery by hand so that when quaternions show up tomorrow as the fix, you understand exactly what problem they solve.

Do

  • Worksheet: given a rocket pitched 20° and yawed 10°, express the gravity vector in the body frame by hand, then confirm in MATLAB.
  • Write MATLAB functions euler2dcm and dcm2euler; round-trip random angles and check the error.
