Final Demo & Briefing

Show it, defend it, and hand off a repository the next cadet can build on.

DAY15

Objectives

  • Deliver MR-3: live bench demo plus a 20-minute briefing covering architecture, models, results, and the sim-vs-hardware comparison.
  • Close the briefing with a concrete "path to flight" segment — what remains before this vehicle could attempt free flight (6DOF validation, ignition/abort logic, structural work, range safety).
  • Leave the repository in a state a future student could clone and continue: README, parameter files, wiring notes, and the notebook complete.
Primer Fifteen days ago this began with a block diagram of a loop that didn't exist yet; today that loop runs live, on the bench, on hardware you brought up yourself. The final demo and briefing are not a new deliverable so much as the moment you narrate the story the last two weeks already told: why a TVC rocket needs active control, how attitude is represented and propagated, how the dynamics and torque model were derived and verified, how the controller was tuned and then made robust to actuator limits, discrete time, and sensor noise, and what changed — and why — going from simulation to real hardware. The briefing closes with a "path to flight" segment that is deliberately honest about what this benchtop study did not prove: full 6DOF behavior, ignition and abort logic, structural qualification, range safety. Naming those gaps explicitly is good engineering, not an admission of failure. The last task — leaving the repository, parameter files, wiring notes, and notebook in a state a future student could clone and pick up cold — is the same discipline that made Phase 3 possible in the first place: nothing here worked because of a clever last-minute trick, it worked because each day built cleanly on a well-documented previous one.

Milestone Review · Day 15

MR-3 — Flight Software Demo

Ready when you can check every box:

  • Flight computer holds its loop rate with sensors, estimator, controller, servo output, and logging all active.
  • Estimator validated at known angles and characterized for drift.
  • Live demo: tilt the assembly, gimbal corrects — correct direction, sensible magnitude, stable.
  • Bench-vs-sim step-response overlay with a defensible explanation of every visible difference.
  • 20-minute briefing delivered; repository handoff-ready.
  • Can answer: What surprised you most going from sim to hardware? Which single model improvement would most reduce the sim/bench gap? What is the very next test on the path to a real flight?
